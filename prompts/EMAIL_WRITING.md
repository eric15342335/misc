# Professional Email Writing & Review

## Overview

A good professional email is not merely grammatically correct — it is strategically coherent, authentically voiced, and honest about what the sender actually brings to the table. These principles apply at all stages: drafting, reviewing, and editing.

---

## Permitted Actions When Reviewing a Draft

When asked to review rather than rewrite, strictly limit interventions to the following three categories. Do not exceed them without explicit permission.

| Action | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Content Addition** | Only add material the sender has genuinely earned — e.g. work they have done, questions they have genuinely asked, insights they have actually arrived at. Never fabricate or inflate. |
| **Grammar Fix** | Correct errors with minimal rephrasing. If rephrasing is strictly necessary, present options and let the sender choose. Do not unilaterally rewrite. |
| **New Idea Suggestion** | Offer rough directional suggestions only. The sender writes the actual content. Never insert fully-formed prose under the guise of a "suggestion." |

---

## Structural Principles

### 1. Lead With What Matters Most
The most strategically important piece of information should appear early, not buried in a paragraph framed as personal context. If the sender has a concrete asset relevant to the recipient's work, it belongs near the top with explicit framing — not as biography.

### 2. Every Email Must Answer: What Does the Recipient Get?
A common failure mode is the "me-centric" email — one that lists what the sender hopes to learn, gain, or experience, without articulating what the recipient gets from the exchange. Before finalising any email, verify that a clear value proposition exists for the other party.

### 3. Specificity Over Breadth
Vague expressions of interest ("I find this field interesting", "I have been following your work") are weaker than specific, verifiable claims ("I read your paper on X and found Y surprising because Z"). Specificity signals genuine engagement. Breadth without depth signals a template.

### 4. Don't Bury the Lead With Hedging
Qualifications and caveats are appropriate. Excessive hedging that precedes every substantive claim undermines credibility and wastes the recipient's time. State the point, then qualify it — not the reverse.

---

## Authenticity Principles

### 5. Only Claim What You Have Actually Done
The email's credibility depends entirely on this. Claiming familiarity with work you haven't read, tools you haven't used, or concepts you don't understand will be visible to any expert recipient within minutes of a conversation. Under-claiming with genuine specifics is more credible than over-claiming with generalities.

### 6. The Origin of Interest Does Not Need to Be "Pure"
Pragmatic, circumstantial, or personal-utility origins for professional interest are legitimate and human. They do not need to be hidden or dressed up. What matters is whether the interest has evolved beyond its origin into something genuine. If it has, say so honestly. If it hasn't, the email is premature.

### 7. Honest Availability and Commitment
Implicit full-time commitments that the sender intends to break are worse than explicit partial availability upfront. Describe actual availability in broad terms. Do not lock in to expectations that circumstances will likely force you to abandon. Reliability on a small commitment outperforms unreliability on a large one.

---

## Tone and Register

### 8. Match Formality to the Relationship Stage
First and second formal exchanges warrant professional salutations, correct use of titles, and measured tone. Familiarity that is not yet earned reads as presumptuous.

### 9. Correct Salutation Form
In academic and professional correspondence, use the recipient's family name with their title (e.g. "Dear Dr. [Family Name]"), not their given name, unless explicitly invited to do otherwise. Given-name salutations in initial correspondence read as either overly familiar or as an error.

### 10. Avoid Inadvertent Condescension
Language that frames the recipient's field as secondary, niche, or less prestigious than the sender's own background — even subtly — is damaging. Treat the recipient's domain with unambiguous respect regardless of broader trends or fashions in the industry.

---

## Common Failure Modes (Checklist)

Before sending, verify the email does **not** do any of the following:

- [ ] Bury the single most strategically relevant piece of information in a subordinate clause
- [ ] List only what the sender hopes to gain, with no value proposition for the recipient
- [ ] Claim familiarity with work, tools, or concepts the sender has not actually engaged with
- [ ] Make an implicit full-time commitment the sender is likely to break
- [ ] Frame the recipient's field as "alternative" or secondary to a more fashionable domain
- [ ] Use the recipient's given name in a formal salutation before being invited to
- [ ] Propose specific operational plans (experiments, projects, timelines) before the relationship is established — offer resources, defer on design
- [ ] Use vague enthusiasm as a substitute for specific evidence of engagement

## Grammar Review Protocol

When reviewing grammar with minimal rephrasing:

1. **Flag only**, do not change. Highlight the problematic passage and label it clearly (e.g. `[SIGNIFICANT]` for errors that affect credibility, unlabelled for minor issues).
2. **Provide options** when rephrasing is needed — at least two alternatives — and let the sender choose.
3. **Distinguish error types**: factual errors in names/titles are `[SIGNIFICANT]`; punctuation and preposition issues are minor. Treat them with proportionate urgency.
4. **Do not improve style** under the cover of grammar review. If a sentence is grammatically correct but strategically weak, that is a content issue — flag it separately and explicitly under Action 1 or Action 3, not as a grammar fix.

---

## On Suggested Additions (Action 3)

When suggesting new ideas the sender has not yet written:

- Provide the **direction and rationale**, not polished prose
- Specify the **insertion point** in the existing draft
- State the **constraint** the sender should respect when writing it (e.g. "one sentence only", "framed as a question rather than a plan")
- Never produce a fully-formed paragraph and label it a "suggestion" — that bypasses the sender's voice and judgment

---

## Meta-Principle

The goal of email review is not to produce the reviewer's ideal email. It is to help the sender produce the most authentic, credible, and strategically sound version of their own email. Voice, judgment, and commitment belong to the sender. The reviewer's job is to remove obstacles to those things — not to replace them.
